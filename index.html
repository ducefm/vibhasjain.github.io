Chyme

Spin tracks for friends

I love discovering new music. Especially when its from an artist I haven’t heard before. Even more so when they’re from a small town in Australia and have 300 followers. The hipster in me rejoices at having chanced upon something deliciously indie and niche.

I get a real kick out of recommending tracks to friends who don’t hunt for music as proactively. Some of them have similar tastes and really enjoy my picks. Unlike me, they don’t constantly trawl the internet looking for tunes.

I enjoy introducing my friends to music they love.

As do many music enthusiasts like me. The internet is littered with blogs, collections and playlists carefully curated by heavy listeners. These people are taste makers; they hand pick fresh music before it becomes popular and help it spread.

Curating music is frustrating when I don’t get feedback from my listeners. This is what a curator typically experiences:

Feedback is lost while switching platforms


Feedback is often lost while jumping between social media, messaging and music apps. This isn’t a particularly rewarding experience for curators. Curators need to know which songs were well received and which ones tanked.

At first, I tried to solve this with an app named Pick



Pick - Share music easy

We had a lot of fun building Pick, but it didn't quite hit the spot. While we were able to share songs, there was a considerable lag between when we shared songs and when the reciever listened to them. This led us to ask:

How can we listen to music together in real time?

We envisioned an experience where two listeners could go back and forth playing tracks for each other

Hush

Building Spatial Awareness

Housing.com helps people find places to live. Typically our customers browse a series of listings, look at photographs, read through specifications and schedule a property viewing to close the deal.




Typical Browsing Experience - Zooming in on images


They don’t get an up close and personal view of the house until they visit it. Photographs don’t necessarily give them the spatial awareness they need to make a decision. Moreover photographs don’t help the customer form a mental floor map.


We want our customers to make faster, more informed decisions. Visiting a property only to be disappointed by shabby housing is a poor consumer experience. So we defined our problem statement as:

How can we give customers a rich preview of a house?


How can we help customers form this mental map?


Startups like Matterport attempt to tackle this problem by 3D Mapping interiors. While effective, this solution is too expensive to scale. Housing.com hosts thousands of listings. We chose instead to experiment with walkthrough videos. Well shot videos let the user lean back and experience the space virtually. However interaction with videos is limited to play and pause which constricts the user to an IKEA like path they have to follow.





We want the user to thoroughly explore different rooms. They should be able to quickly and repeatedly switch between rooms. This should familiarize the user with the space enough so they can comfortably find their way around the house when they visit.



First iOS Prototype - Swipe to reveal navigation cards





While this works, we also want to correlate each room to its position on the floor map. Our second prototype achieves that by highlighting each room on the map as it scrolls by.



Second iOS Prototype includes floor map




While we've used this prototype to navigate a house, we're sure it has many other applications. We've open sourced the code on Github for you to play with!

Kyte

Light speed browsing experience

Along the way I also built Kyte — A browser plugin which loads your favorite websites every time you open a new tab. You can use arrow keys to quickly switch between websites you visit everyday. It would open one of them randomly every time you open a new tab. The ideas was that you would be caught up with them more often without having to constanly check them. Plus you could consume them in their original design instead of a cookie cutter RSS reader.

Kyte is no longer on the Chrome extension store but we had a lot of fun building and shipping it. We managed about 40 users before we moved on to building Pick.The newest version of Safari incorporates something similar.
